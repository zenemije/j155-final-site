Artivism in San Francisco

San Francisco is famously known to be one of the most artistic metropolitan cities in the world. Murals line the streets from corner to corner of this 7x7 mile creative hub.

The video below offers just a glimpe into the vibrant imagery you'll find in San Francisco's Mission District, one of the most visually stunning neighborhoods in the Bay Area.

"Alto al Fuego en la Misión", 2019

The murals of San Francisco’s Mission District have come to evoke bittersweet emotions within the community these days. While the vibrant colors of fresh paint are known to be a staple of pride in the neighborhood, the incessant loss of life at the hands of police brutality serves as one of the most common inspirations for these murals that seem to be sprouting more and more frequently. One of the latest was unveiled in November of last year on the busy corner of 24th and Capp Street just above the Calle24 Latino Cultural District offices.

"Alto al Fuego en La Misión" began as a mural solely dedicated to Amilcar Perez Lopez, a Ch’orti’ Mayan immigrant from Guatemala and beloved Mission resident who was wrongfully murdered by the SFPD on February 26, 2015. What’s grown from this project, however, has become an expansive altar for honoring several Black and Brown folks who had been slain at the hands of police and border officers over the past couple of years. The additional lives depicted in the mural are that of Mario Woods, Alex Nieto, Luis Gongora Pat, Jesus Adolfo Delgado, Roxana Hernandez, Claudia Patricia Gomez González and Oscar and Valeria Martinez.

Father Richard of the Justice4Amilcar Coalition, in collaboration with Amilcar’s family, Precita Eyes Muralists, Mission Housing Development Corporation and Homies Organizing the Mission to Empower Youth (HOMEY) initiated the 2-year-long process. Recruited artists Carla Elana Wojczuk, Lucía González Ippolito, Flavia Elisa Mora, Cristian Muńoz, Anna Lisa Escobedo, Adrianna Adams, Pancho Pescador and Sonia G. Molina brought the vision to life.

The mural is oriented as a retablo—a stoic portrait of Amilcar on one side and a portrayal of Amiclar’s family holding a message on the other. The message reads “Familia Perez Lopez declare justice for the assassination of our son, assassinated 26/02/15 by the police of San Francisco, CA. Amilcar Perez.” Amid the rolling landscapes transitioning from Guatemala to San Francisco lie novena candles with portraits of others who were wrongfully killed, their faces illuminating the dark consequences of militarization in Black and Brown communities.

Lucía González Ippolito, one of the lead muralists, shines a light on some of the symbolism embedded within the mural’s imagery: “Julia, a lead member of HOMEY and danzante, is depicted here holding a copal next to Amilcar’s family. Her image was created to serve as our version of the Statue of Liberty. On the other side of Amilcar’s family, we have our Lady Justice represented as a migrant woman holding her child while crossing the border.”

Ippolito also points out the inspiration taken from her mother Juana Alicia’s “Alto al Fuego” mural painted in the Mission District in 1988. The mural was created as a response to the killings in Central America which occurred as a direct result of U.S. military intervention.The piece depicted hands held up against paramilitary guns in a stance of resistance: “We wanted to recreate the guns from her mural as SFPD guns instead with the badge number of the officer responsible for Amilcar’s death.”

Below is a clip from an interview with Flavia Elisa Mora and her thoughts on what role art can play in institutional accountability and community healing.

The hope behind the mural’s creation is that the community feels informed and encouraged to educate and arm themselves against the systemic violence imposed on Black and Brown bodies.

Fellow muralist Flavia Elisa Mora shares her thoughts on the role that she hopes the mural plays in achieving accountability within the community: “It’s not a new thing for Black and Brown folk to be targeted by the police and military forces in general. It’s something that continues to be an issue and the system unfortunately fails to be accountable for it; however, I think through art, we are able to create a platform for certain issues to be seen and to also honor the lives affected by them.”
